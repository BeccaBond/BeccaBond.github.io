Narwhal-Inspired Play Experience

Summary

My four classmates and I created this strategy game inspired by narwhals' navigation and hunting behavior to engage elementary school students. We collaborated closely to design the complex game while taking into account student safety, time and area limits, and mass part production. Through the process, I grew my skills in technical design and team collaboration.

After six weeks of detailed design, fabrication and play-testing, we were selected out of twenty-five student teams to host our game in the Computer Science Education Weekend at the Museum of Science in Boston! The students had a lot of fun playing narwhals and I was honored to share our project with the next generation of engineers.

Gameplay: You're a narwhal tasked with finding food for your blessing but you got lost! Using your tusk, navigate the waters to find your way back home and catch fish so you don't starve.

Game Design & Creation

We chose a distinctive behavior and corresponding motion of narwhals to inspire our bio-inspired play experience. Narwhals in nature use their tusks to sense water temperature, pressure and salinity, testing that the water is safe for travel. We incorporated this behavior into our game by building a memory maze in which players must use their tusks to test the water and find a safe path home. Narwhals also use their tusks to stun fish while hunting, which we incorporated by magnetically suspending cloth fish above the maze grid for players to whack down and "eat" in narwhal-style baskets.

My individual task was to design the boxes that hid the navigation cards then popped the cards up for viewing when a player tested the "water" with a tusk. For the pop-up mechanism, I took inspiration from a toaster and designed a lever which popped the card up when pressed and let the card fall again when released. I designed the boxes using sheet metal CAD tools and laser cut and folded the dielines into boxes.

My most challenging task was mass producing pop-up boxes to fill our 5x5 grid, plus extra boxes in the case that some broke during play. With our demonstration day quickly approaching, I laser cut dielines, cut navigation cards, and 3D printed levers and card sliders to assemble more than thirty pop-up navigation boxes.

Team Collaboration & Project Requirements

My team of five maximized productivity by dividing tasks among ourselves and maintaining consistent communication on tasks' status over six weeks. We held plus-deltas and respectfully discussed conflicts or obstacles that arose. Finally, we met the project requirements of game area, materials budget, and student safety and accessibility. Through close collaboration, we engaged elementary students in an entertaining and interactive story about narwhals.

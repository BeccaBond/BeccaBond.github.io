Flying Fish Automaton

Summary

This push-activated flying fish was part of a three-week mechanical challenge to design & build a nature-inspired automaton using at least one motion-converting mechanism.

Inspired by the flying fish, my automaton utilized an angled rack & pinion to convert a linear force to synchronized rotational motion of the fish's pectoral & pelvic fins.

Project results:

  • Inspired by the four-winged flying fish
  • Two angled racks and four pinions convert linear force to synchronized rotational motion of the pectoral & pelvic fins.
  • Two elastic bands return the device to its initial position when a force is released; an adjustable distance screw allows for calibration of the elastics' lengths and strengths.
  • Hand-carved airfoil shape and symmetrically angled fins create aesthetic quality for the fish.

Skills developed & practiced:

  • Project scoping
  • Parametric CAD
  • Design for assembly
  • Gear design & systematizing
  • Woodworking & carving
  • Laser cutting & 3D printing

Design

Created proportional & scalable body dimensions by drawing from existing sketches and writing variable dimensions as percentages of the fish's chord length.

Fish body dimensions calculated as percentages of expected chord length.

Calculated the pitch diameters for the pectoral & pelvic spurs based on the distance the rack travels within the fish's body and the fractions of a revolution each wing must rotate to fold out.

Pelvic wings need to rotate further than pectoral wings since they fold in closer to the center of the fish, therefore their spurs should have smaller pitch diameters.

P = d/Fπ where d = rack travel distance (.5 in), F = fraction of rotation (102/360 & 114/360 for pectoral & pelvic spurs, respectively).

Challenges & Learnings

I initially had doubts about my ability to craft the fish's body by hand. Wood carving was a great experience, but it meant I had to redefine a lot of my dimensions to fit the carved body instead of the original model, which I could have easily 3D printed.

To overcome this obstacle, I wrote my dimensions in CAD as variable percentages of set measurements, such as the fish's chord length or the depth of the wing slots, measurements which depended entirely on the results of my carving. After carving, I simply set these measurements in CAD and was able to maintain the correct proportions for the other parts in fabrication.

A second challenge I had was finding the balance between weak enough elastics to allow a low-effort force to move the automaton, yet strong enough to pull it back to its original position. To overcome this challenge, I designed an adjustable screw mechanism in the corner of the box, which allowed me to precisely adjust how far the lower elastic was stretched and therefore how much force it exerted on the device.

I also included a second elastic higher on the rod (the rod is the moving part coming out from under the fish, which connects to the racks inside the fish). A single elastic on the lower part of the rod created a lever-like mechanism with an applied force on the middle of the rod, straining the rack and hindering its sliding movement. I added the second elastic to the top of the rod to balance the rod and keep it perpendicular to the ground.
